\vspace{1em}

A bicycle wheel rim lashed to a bamboo pole - this is the lightning protection system being promoted across certain Indian villages. Electrical engineers have concluded not only that it does not work, but that it may actually endanger the lives of the people standing near it. The promoters appear to mean well. The communities they serve face real, lethal risk. Certified alternatives cost money many of these households simply do not have. And yet good intentions do not settle the question of whether advocacy for a dangerous device can be ethically defended. This essay argues that it cannot. Examined through a consequentialist lens, the device's expected harm outweighs any speculative benefit. Deontologically, advocacy for it violates the duties of honesty, care, and competence owed to the people it claims to protect. And measured against the ECSA Code of Conduct, no technically qualified person can lend their authority to it in good conscience. The dilemma is genuine - but it resolves, on careful analysis, in one direction.


An effective lightning protection system requires three things: an air terminal, a down conductor, and a ground electrode. The bamboo pole, being an insulator, provides none of the last two, which means any strike to the rim has no controlled path to earth and may instead discharge toward nearby people or structures. The device does not merely fail to protect - it may redirect harm. What makes this harder to see is that lightning strike density in India is low enough that the device could go years without being struck at all, and that silence is easily, and falsely, read as proof that it works.


Consequentialism holds that the morality of an action is determined entirely by its outcomes - whether it produces the greatest good for the greatest number (Mill, 1863). A fair consequentialist assessment means genuinely engaging with the best case for advocacy before deciding whether it stands.

That case is not trivial. These communities are poor, face genuine lethal risk from lightning, and lack access to certified protection systems. Peter Singer's work on global obligation suggests that where people face life-threatening risk and wealthier institutions have failed to provide solutions, improvised local responses deserve moral credit rather than condemnation (Singer, 1972). If the device, even as a psychological anchor, caused residents to remain indoors during storms, some deaths may be prevented. If the alternative is not a certified rod but nothing at all, a marginal intervention carries weight. This argument has genuine force.

The consequentialist defence depends critically, however, on the device being at minimum non-harmful. That assumption fails. The missing down conductor and ground electrode mean that a lightning strike on the rim produces uncontrolled current discharge in the immediate vicinity of the structure. The expected value is therefore not merely zero; it is plausibly negative, because the device may attract a strike and then fail to dissipate it safely. Consequentialism takes seriously the risk of rare but catastrophic harm to large groups (Parfit, 1984), and claiming an entire village is protected when it is not does exactly that.

The most subtle consequentialist harm is the behavioural effect of false security. If villagers believe their community is protected, they may remain outdoors during storms, congregate near the structure, or abandon the vigilance that might otherwise have kept them safe. This is made worse by the fact that lightning strikes are rare enough that the device may go years without being tested, building a false reputation that keeps communities stuck with something that does not work.

A third harm is opportunity cost. Promotion of the device consumes community trust, local leadership attention, and whatever advocacy capacity exists - resources that could instead support evidence-based shelter protocols or pressure on government bodies to subsidise certified equipment. A false solution does not merely fail to help; it occupies the space where real help could grow. Therefore, the consequentialist verdict is negative: the expected harm from advocacy far outweighs any speculative placebo benefit.


Deontological ethics judges an action not by the resulting outcome but by whether it respects people's right to make informed decisions and upholds basic moral duties (Kant, 1785). It offers a distinct - and in some respects more fundamental - critique of advocacy for the bicycle wheel device, organised around three duties: honesty, care, and competence.

The duty of honesty is the most immediate. If the device is presented as standards-compliant or reliably protective for an entire village, while the advocate lacks evidence that it works safely, advocacy constitutes material misrepresentation - even if unintentional. Nagabhushana et al.\ (2020) explicitly state that the arrangement cannot work the way it has been made out. To promote it in those terms treats community members not as persons entitled to truthful information bearing on their survival, but as instruments for a narrative. In Kantian terms, this fails the second formulation of the categorical imperative: to treat humanity never merely as a means, but always also as an end in itself (Kant, 1785). The villagers are being deceived rather than informed, their trust leveraged rather than honoured.

The duty of care provides a second basis for criticism. Deontology asks not only whether harm occurred, but whether the act itself put others at unnecessary risk in a way that violates a basic duty of care. Endorsing a safety device carries an implied warranty of reliability that ordinary speech does not. The knowledge imbalance here is significant: community members cannot independently assess whether a bamboo pole constitutes a safe down conductor. That asymmetry, familiar from the literature on professional ethics (Davis, 1998), is precisely what generates heightened duties of care in technical domains. By promoting the device without disclosing its known limitations, the advocate places people in elevated danger while depriving them of the knowledge that would allow them to respond accordingly.

The duty of competence provides a third ground. Advocating an engineering safety solution carries implied technical authority that ordinary speech does not. If the advocate is not qualified to assess lightning protection design, the advocacy itself becomes ethically blameworthy independent of outcome and independent of intent. This is different from lying: someone can genuinely believe in the device while being unqualified to assess it, but sincerity alone does not make the claim acceptable.

It is worth noting, however, that deontology draws a distinction between acting in good faith out of ignorance and continuing to advocate once the facts are known. Someone who promoted the device without knowing it was dangerous cannot be blamed in the same way, and deserves that allowance. But once the technical failure is publicly established, continued advocacy involves knowingly providing false safety information, and no quality of will survives that threshold. Kant's first formulation supports this conclusion: the principle that where certified safety equipment is unavailable it is permissible to recommend an unverified alternative that creates the appearance of protection cannot be universalised without undermining the institution of safety advice - the very institution on which vulnerable communities most depend.

Where consequentialism condemns the device because of the harms it produces, deontology condemns advocacy for it because of the duties it violates, regardless of whether any particular strike ever occurs.


The ECSA Code of Conduct, Rule 3.3(a), requires registered persons to at all times give priority to the health, safety and interest of the public (ECSA, 2017). When safety is at stake, it takes priority - full stop. An important question is whether Rule 3.3(a) applies where the advocates are not registered engineers but informal community actors. It applies with full force to any technically qualified person who lends credibility to the device, and with partial force to anyone who presents themselves as having technical authority.

Engineering expertise carries social weight precisely because the public cannot evaluate technical claims independently. To invoke that authority in support of a hazardous device betrays the trust the profession has built (Davis, 1998). Rule 3.3(b) reinforces this with a positive obligation: where professional advice is not accepted, registered persons must inform the relevant party of any consequences detrimental to public health and safety, and notify the Council accordingly (ECSA, 2017). Where communities remain unprotected and no authority is acting, the obligation is not to endorse an unsafe substitute, but to escalate the problem and offer honest guidance on the safest available behaviours in the meantime.

The preceding analysis might appear to resolve the dilemma too cleanly. The communities that adopted this device face lightning fatalities, limited infrastructure, and in many cases indifference from the state. A strand of harm reduction ethics would argue that some protective gesture, even a flawed one, is preferable to none if it motivates cautious behaviour (Faden and Powers, 2008). And there is a deontological argument that condemning informal safety efforts in resource-poor contexts without providing alternatives imposes obligations on the vulnerable that the affluent have never had to bear.

These arguments do not ultimately prevail. The harm reduction case fails because the device is not merely imperfect - it is under certain conditions actively hazardous. The deontological argument about unequal obligations is a legitimate critique of structural inequality, but it does not follow that the response is to endorse a dangerous device - it follows that the inequality itself must be challenged. What both frameworks support is not a choice between the bicycle wheel and nothing, but a refusal to accept that as the only available choice.

This essay set out to determine whether advocacy for the bicycle wheel lightning protection device can be ethically defended once the technical facts are known. It cannot. The consequentialist case - that something is better than nothing for communities with no alternatives - is coherent, but fails because the device's expected value is negative: it generates false assurance, potential physical harm on the occasion of a strike, and opportunity costs that displace more effective interventions. The deontological case confirms this: advocacy violates the duties of honesty, care, and competence owed to the people it claims to protect, regardless of intent. The ECSA Code of Conduct, specifically Rule 3.3(a)'s absolute priority of public safety, leaves no room for a qualified person to endorse the device in good conscience.

The deeper failure here, however, is not in the choices of people facing genuine danger with no resources, but in the poverty, state neglect, and lack of access to technical knowledge that narrowed their options so severely. To judge the bicycle wheel without questioning those conditions is to treat the symptom while ignoring the disease.

A bicycle wheel rim lashed to a bamboo pole - this is the lightning protection system being promoted across certain Indian villages. Electrical engineers have concluded not only that it does not work, but that it may actually endanger the lives of the people standing near it. The promoters appear to mean well: the communities they serve face real, lethal risk, and certified alternatives cost money that many of these households simply do not have. However, good intentions do not settle the question of whether advocacy for a dangerous device can be ethically defended. This essay argues that it cannot. Examined through a consequentialist lens, the device's expected harm outweighs any speculative benefit. Deontologically, advocacy for it violates the duties of honesty, care, and competence owed to the people it claims to protect. Measured against the ECSA Code of Conduct, no technically qualified person can lend their authority to it in good conscience. The dilemma is genuine - but it resolves, on careful analysis, in one direction.

An effective lightning protection system requires three things: an air terminal, a down conductor, and a ground electrode (Nagabhushana et al., 2020). The bamboo pole, being an insulator, provides none of the last two, which means any strike on the rim has no controlled path to earth and may instead discharge toward nearby people or structures. The device does not merely fail to protect - it may redirect harm. What makes this harder to see is that any one structure is unlikely to be struck in a given year, meaning the device could go years without being struck at all, and that silence is easily and falsely read as proof that it works.


Consequentialism holds that the morality of an action is determined entirely by its outcomes - whether it produces the greatest good for the greatest number (Mill, 1863). A fair consequentialist assessment means genuinely engaging with the best case for advocacy before deciding whether it stands.

That case is not trivial. These communities are poor, face genuine lethal risk from lightning, and lack access to certified protection systems. Peter Singer's work on global obligation suggests that where people face life-threatening risk and wealthier institutions have failed to provide solutions, improvised local responses deserve moral credit rather than condemnation (Singer, 1972). If the device, even as a psychological anchor, caused residents to remain indoors during storms, some deaths might be prevented. If the alternative is not a certified rod but nothing at all, a marginal intervention carries weight. This argument has genuine force.

The consequentialist defence depends critically, however, on the device being at least non-harmful. That assumption fails. The missing down conductor and ground electrode mean that a lightning strike on the rim produces an uncontrolled discharge of current in the immediate vicinity of the structure. The expected value is therefore not merely zero; it is plausibly negative, because the device may attract a strike and then fail to dissipate it safely. Consequentialism takes seriously the risk of rare but catastrophic harm to large groups (Parfit, 1984), and claiming that an entire village is protected when it is not does exactly that.

The most subtle consequentialist harm is the behavioural effect of false security. If villagers believe their community is protected, they may remain outdoors during storms, congregate near the structure, or abandon the vigilance that might otherwise have kept them safe. This is made worse by the fact that the probability of any one structure being struck in a given year is low enough that the device may go years without being tested, building a false reputation that keeps communities stuck with something that does not work.

A third harm is opportunity cost. Promotion of the device consumes community trust, local leadership attention, and whatever advocacy capacity exists - resources that could instead support evidence-based shelter protocols or pressure on government bodies to subsidise certified equipment. A false solution does not merely fail to help; it occupies the space where real help could grow. Therefore, the consequentialist verdict is negative: the expected harm from advocacy far outweighs any speculative placebo benefit.


Deontological ethics judges an action not by the resulting outcome but by whether it respects people's right to make informed decisions and upholds basic moral duties (Kant, 1785). It offers a distinct critique of advocacy for the bicycle wheel device, organised around three duties: honesty, care, and competence.

The duty of honesty is the most immediate. If the device is presented as standards-compliant or reliably protective for an entire village, while the advocate lacks evidence that it works safely, advocacy constitutes a material misrepresentation - even if unintentional. Nagabhushana et al.\ (2020) explicitly state that the arrangement cannot work as it has been made out to be. To promote it in those terms treats community members not as persons entitled to truthful information bearing on their survival, but as instruments for a narrative. In Kantian terms, this fails the second formulation of the categorical imperative: to treat humanity never merely as a means, but always also as an end in itself (Kant, 1785). The villagers are being deceived rather than informed, their trust leveraged rather than honoured.

The duty of care provides a second basis for criticism. Deontology asks not only whether harm occurred, but whether the act itself put others at unnecessary risk in a way that violates a basic duty of care. Endorsing a safety device carries an implied warranty of reliability that ordinary speech does not. The knowledge imbalance here is significant: community members cannot independently assess whether a bamboo pole constitutes a safe down conductor. That asymmetry, familiar from the literature on professional ethics (Davis, 1998), is precisely what generates heightened duties of care in technical domains. By promoting the device without disclosing its known limitations, the advocate places people in elevated danger while depriving them of the knowledge that would allow them to respond accordingly.

The duty of competence provides a third ground. Advocating an engineering safety solution carries implied technical authority that ordinary speech does not. If the advocate is not qualified to assess lightning protection design, the advocacy itself becomes ethically blameworthy independent of outcome and independent of intent. This is different from lying: someone can genuinely believe in the device while being unqualified to assess it, but sincerity alone does not make the claim acceptable.

It is worth noting, however, that deontology draws a distinction between acting in good faith out of ignorance and continuing to advocate once the facts are known. Someone who promoted the device without knowing it was dangerous cannot be blamed in the same way, and deserves that allowance. But once the technical failure is publicly established, continued advocacy involves knowingly providing false safety information, and no quality of will survives that threshold. Kant's first formulation supports this conclusion: the principle that where certified safety equipment is unavailable it is permissible to recommend an unverified alternative that creates the appearance of protection cannot be universalised without undermining the institution of safety advice - the very institution on which vulnerable communities most depend.

Where consequentialism condemns the device because of the harms it produces, deontology condemns advocacy for it because of the duties it violates, regardless of whether any particular strike ever occurs.


The ECSA Code of Conduct, Rule 3.3(a), requires registered persons to give priority at all times to the health, safety, and interest of the public (ECSA, 2017). When safety is at stake, it takes priority - full stop. An important question is whether Rule 3.3(a) applies where the advocates are not registered engineers but informal community actors. It applies with full force to any technically qualified person who lends credibility to the device, and with partial force to anyone who presents themselves as having technical authority.

Engineering expertise carries social weight precisely because the public cannot evaluate technical claims independently. To invoke that authority in support of a hazardous device betrays the trust the profession has built (Davis, 1998). Rule 3.3(b) reinforces this with a positive obligation: where professional advice is not accepted, registered persons must inform the relevant party of any consequences detrimental to public health and safety, and notify the Council accordingly (ECSA, 2017). Where communities remain unprotected and no authority is acting, the obligation is not to endorse an unsafe substitute, but to escalate the problem and offer honest guidance on the safest available behaviours in the meantime.

The preceding analysis might appear to resolve the dilemma too cleanly. The communities that adopted this device face lightning fatalities, limited infrastructure, and in many cases indifference from the state. A strand of harm reduction ethics would argue that some protective gesture, even a flawed one, is preferable to none if it motivates cautious behaviour (Faden and Powers, 2008). And there is a deontological argument that condemning informal safety efforts in resource-poor contexts without providing alternatives imposes obligations on the vulnerable that the affluent have never had to bear.

These arguments have substance, but they do not ultimately prevail. The harm reduction case fails because the device is not merely imperfect - it is under certain conditions, actively hazardous. The deontological argument about unequal obligations is a legitimate critique of structural inequality, but it does not follow that the response is to endorse a dangerous device - it follows that the inequality itself must be challenged. What both frameworks support is not a choice between the bicycle wheel and nothing, but a refusal to accept that as the only available choice.

This essay set out to determine whether advocacy for the bicycle wheel lightning protection device can be ethically defended once the technical facts are known. It cannot. The consequentialist case - that something is better than nothing for communities with no alternatives - is coherent, but fails because the device's expected value is negative: it generates false assurance, potential physical harm on the occasion of a strike, and opportunity costs that displace more effective interventions. The deontological case confirms this: advocacy violates the duties of honesty, care, and competence owed to the people it claims to protect, regardless of intent. The ECSA Code of Conduct, specifically Rule 3.3(a)'s absolute priority of public safety, leaves no room for a qualified person to endorse the device in good conscience.

The deeper failure here, however, is not in the choices of people facing genuine danger with no resources, but in the poverty, state neglect, and lack of access to technical knowledge that narrowed their options so severely. To judge the bicycle wheel without questioning those conditions is to treat the symptom while ignoring the disease.
